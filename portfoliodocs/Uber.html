Improving the Rideshare Experience for Blind & Low‑Vision Users

Designing accessible rideshare experiences that empower independence through co‑designed solutions, transparent communication, and inclusive app flows.

Timeline 6 weeks
Team Mehak Munir, Edric Delleola, Jordyn Smith, Ashwini Walavalkar
Role UX Researcher & Accessibility Analyst
Tools Zoom, FigJam, NotebookLM, Google Docs

Overview

We set out to understand how blind and low‑vision (BLV) users experience ridesharing and to design inclusive solutions that improve independence, safety, and trust. Through four in‑depth interviews and thematic analysis, we uncovered patterns of communication breakdowns, pick‑up and drop‑off confusion, and driver bias. Together with participants, we co‑designed haptic and audio features, transparent status updates, and driver training pathways.

Background & Motivation

Ridesharing promises autonomy, yet BLV riders frequently face barriers: silent drivers, language mismatches, unclear arrival cues, and guide‑dog refusals. Existing literature often describes problems without prescribing tangible fixes. Our goal was to translate lived experiences into actionable, system‑level design improvements.

Design lens: Inclusive design with an emphasis on trust, dignity, and autonomy.

Research Question

How might we redesign rideshare experiences so that users who are blind or have low vision can travel independently, confidently, and safely?

Participants

We interviewed four completely blind Uber users who regularly rely on ridesharing. Sessions were conducted remotely via Zoom.

Methodology

Approach

Semi‑structured, 60‑minute interviews via Zoom with two moderators (lead + note‑taker). We mapped end‑to‑end ride journeys, surfaced pain points, and invited participants to ideate solutions.

Analysis

Transcripts were coded through a hybrid strategy: AI‑assisted clustering (NotebookLM) followed by manual thematic synthesis in FigJam to preserve nuance and emotion.

Key Findings

Theme 01

Communication Breakdowns

Silent or non‑English communication, limited updates, and inaccessible support eroded confidence mid‑journey.

“He didn’t speak English… I got out of the car and didn’t even know where I was.” — P1
Theme 02

Pick‑Up & Drop‑Off Confusion

Generic arrival messages lacked directionality; drivers requested visual cues; GPS inaccuracies led to unsafe or wrong drop‑offs.

Theme 03

Driver Behavior & Bias

Instances of rudeness, disputing locations, or guide‑dog refusals produced anxiety and reduced independence.

Co‑Designed Solutions

Haptic Feedback Alerts

Phone vibrations intensify as the driver approaches to help locate vehicles without relying on sight or sound.

En‑Route Audio Updates

Spoken cues for streets, turns, and landmarks provide continuous spatial awareness and reduce uncertainty.

Pre‑Pick‑Up Transparency

Frequent, contextual statuses (e.g., “stuck at rail crossing”, “2 min away”) set expectations and lower anxiety.

Driver Accessibility Training

Onboarding modules covering guide‑dog policy, pick‑up etiquette, and communication standards build empathy and consistency.

Discussion & Impact

Three Recommendations

  • Automated Communication: Adaptive, multilingual notifications and arrival cues build trust.
  • Driver Support & Training: Standardize inclusive behaviors and reduce discrimination.
  • Accessibility Audit: Address unlabeled controls and unclear screen‑reader flows within the app.
Curb‑Cut Effect: These improvements benefit everyone — haptics help in noisy places, audio cues aid situational awareness, and transparency improves all rider–driver interactions.

Future Directions

  1. Compare Uber vs. Lyft accessibility policies and outcomes.
  2. Prototype and usability‑test haptic and audio features with BLV riders.
  3. Expand participant diversity across geography, age, and vision levels.

Reflection

Accessibility is not an add‑on; it’s the foundation of good design. This project deepened my practice of listening first, co‑creating with users, and translating emotion‑rich narratives into practical, testable solutions. Small interventions — a vibration, a voice cue, a clear status — can restore independence and dignity at scale.
