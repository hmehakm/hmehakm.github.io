SHEIN Navigation & Information Architecture Redesign

Restructuring SHEIN’s navigation, content hierarchy, and onboarding flow using card sorting, tree testing, heuristics, and sitemap redesign.

Overview

Why SHEIN’s navigation needed help

SHEIN is a global fast-fashion e-commerce platform with thousands of new items added weekly. While the catalog is extensive, its navigation and information architecture can feel overwhelming, with redundant categories, overlapping labels, and an onboarding flow that doesn’t always feel secure or intuitive. Our goal in this course project was to treat SHEIN as a content and structure problem—not just a visual one.

  • Create a clearer, more logical navigation structure for the website.
  • Remove redundancy and overlap in categories and labels.
  • Design a more secure and trustworthy sign-in / registration flow.
Problem space

Content & IA challenges we focused on

Through our initial review and heuristic analysis, we identified recurring content and navigation issues:

  • Redundant or overlapping categories (e.g., multiple areas for similar items).
  • Ambiguous labels such as “Globe” and unclear placement of items like “Family Outfits”.
  • Inconsistent separation of content (kids’ accessories vs. general accessories).
  • Onboarding and account creation flows that did not clearly communicate security or next steps.
Users

Who we were designing for

We created two primary personas to guide content and navigation decisions:

  • Emma, 23 – Trend-focused student & influencer (Los Angeles)
    Wants trendy, affordable, and ideally sustainable pieces. She values efficiency, hates losing progress, and gets frustrated when redundant categories make it hard to find items quickly.
  • Dave, 27 – Tech enthusiast & busy professional (New York)
    Focused on electronics and gadgets. He expects a streamlined experience and becomes frustrated when disorganized categories make it difficult to find specific items.

These personas helped us stay grounded in real motivations: speed, clarity, and trust while navigating a very dense catalog. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Process

How we evolved the navigation with research

Our process combined multiple IA and usability methods, each feeding into the next:

  • Heuristic analysis of key flows (registering, making a purchase).
  • Hybrid card sorting to understand mental models for category groupings.
  • Tree testing to validate findability of key tasks.
  • Sitemap iterations based on real participant data.
  • Wireframes & task flows for onboarding and purchase journeys.
Research

Card sorting – uncovering mental models

We ran two main rounds of card sorting (plus a pilot), testing up to 35 cards across 10–15 categories with 18 total participants. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

  • What worked: Top-level categories like Women, Men, Kids, Pets, Electronics were intuitive.
  • Where users struggled: Items like Accessories, Language, Globe, and Account often overlapped or felt unclear.
  • Edge cases: “Family Outfits” consistently landed in “I don’t know where this goes,” signaling it deserved its own dedicated category instead of being buried under Kids.

In a later round, we specifically revisited contentious items like “Kids Accessories” and confirmed that our initial placement under Accessories was still acceptable, while “Language” clearly belonged under Account, not a vague “Globe” icon.

Research

Tree testing – validating findability

After restructuring categories conceptually, we used tree testing with 7 participants to check whether users could actually find key items and actions in the revised structure.

  • Account creation: 71.4% successfully found where to create a new account, but confusion remained between labels like “Register,” “Sign Up,” and “Login.”
  • Pet accessories: 85.7% correctly navigated to pet leashes under Accessories with only minor detours.
  • Phone cases & screen protectors: 100% success, suggesting this area of the IA was already quite strong.
  • Holiday decor: 71.4% found home decor easily, but “Home Decor” vs. “Home Textiles” caused confusion for the rest.

Overall, the tree test confirmed that our structure was moving in the right direction, while highlighting where label clarity and category differentiation still needed work. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Information architecture

From messy to intentional: sitemap iterations

We created three main sitemap versions, each informed by research:

  • Version 1: Initial structure based on the pilot card sort and current site, highlighting redundancies and scattered categories.
  • Version 2: Reorganized categories to group related items, merged redundant sections, and created clearer groupings (e.g., giving “Family Outfits” its own space).
  • Version 3 (Final): Removed “Globe” and folded its functions into “Account,” refined labels, and clarified confusing areas revealed by card sort and tree test data.

Together, these iterations turned a scattered taxonomy into a more streamlined, user-aligned structure.

Evaluation

Heuristic analysis & content recommendations

We performed heuristic evaluations on two core tasks: logging in / registering and making a purchase. Both flows averaged around 3.1–3.4 out of 5 on usability and clarity, leaving clear room for improvement. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

  • Consistent labeling: Ensure product labels, categories, and navigation elements use consistent language across all pages.
  • Decluttered interface: Introduce a “hide promotions” or focused mode to reduce banner fatigue during browsing.
  • Simplified filters: Limit filter options to key attributes and add search within filters to reduce cognitive load.
  • Enhanced verification: Implement email/SMS verification for critical actions to increase trust and prevent errors.
  • Guest checkout: Make guest checkout more visible with clear benefits (speed, no account required).
  • Recommended sections & improved search: Add “Trending,” “Most Popular,” and smarter search with synonym handling and typo tolerance.
Design

Wireframes & task flows

Our wireframes were built directly from the final sitemap and heuristic recommendations, focusing on two critical journeys:

  • Task 1 – Sign in / Register: A clearer path to account creation via the Account button, with options to sign in, register via email/phone, or use Google/Facebook. A 6-digit verification code was introduced to reinforce security and build trust in the flow.
  • Task 2 – Make a purchase: A simplified product-to-checkout path, guiding users from product page (size, color, quantity) to cart, and then through a reduced-field checkout with clearer payment, promo code, and notification options.

The goal was not to redesign the entire UI visually, but to ensure workflows and content structure better matched user expectations and reduced friction in core tasks.

Outcomes & reflection

What improved & what I learned

By the end of the project, we had a validated IA, clearer labels, and wireframes that aligned navigation with real user behavior. The work also changed how I think about content and systems:

  • Process thinking over interface thinking: Focusing on flows, labels, and structure before pixels leads to more sustainable decisions.
  • Thinking beyond the current design: Instead of “fixing” small pieces, we stepped back to re-examine the whole taxonomy.
  • Consistency matters: Once we committed to new structures and labels, applying them consistently across the system was key.
  • Recruitment & method tweaks: We refined how we recruited participants and adjusted our card sort approach to get more reliable data.

This project strengthened my interest in content strategy and information architecture, and gave me a repeatable process for restructuring complex navigation problems in future work.
