Content Strategy · Information Architecture

Re-structuring SHEIN’s Navigation for Real People, Not Just Categories

A course project where our team used card sorting, tree testing, heuristics, and wireframing to bring order to SHEIN’s overwhelming navigation and to design a more trustworthy onboarding flow.

Overview

Fast fashion, slow navigation

SHEIN ships clothes and accessories to more than 200 countries and adds thousands of products every week. From a business perspective, that’s exciting. From a navigation perspective, it’s chaos. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

When I looked at the site with my team, we saw redundant categories, vague labels like “Globe,” and scattered content placements that made it easy to get lost. Our goal was not to “re-skin” the UI, but to re-build the underlying information structure so people like Emma and Dave (our personas) could actually find what they came for.

Over the quarter, we:

  • Mapped user needs through personas grounded in real scenarios.
  • Ran multiple rounds of card sorting to understand how people naturally group products.
  • Validated our structure through tree testing and heuristic evaluations.
  • Created a new sitemap and wireframes for registration and purchase flows.
Personas

Designing for Emma and Dave

Before touching categories, we wanted to ground our decisions in real stories. We created two core personas based on research and typical SHEIN user behavior: Emma, a trend-focused student influencer, and Dave, a time-pressed tech enthusiast shopping for electronics. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Persona 01

Emma – “Don’t make me lose my progress”

Emma is a 23-year-old student and social media influencer from Los Angeles. She loves trendy, affordable (and ideally sustainable) fashion.

  • Goal: Find pieces fast, without losing items she already liked.
  • Frustration: Redundant categories and unclear navigation that force her to re-search items.
  • Needs: Clear category labels, predictable navigation, and a feeling that the site “remembers” her path.
Persona 02

Dave – “I just want to find the right gadget”

Dave is a 27-year-old software developer and tech enthusiast. He comes to SHEIN looking for specific electronics and accessories.

  • Goal: Quickly locate and purchase tech products with minimal clicks.
  • Frustration: Disorganized categories and cluttered navigation slow him down.
  • Needs: A streamlined IA that mirrors how he thinks about product types and specs.
Approach

How we broke the problem down

We treated the navigation as a hypothesis to test and iterate on. Instead of guessing category labels, we watched how people grouped and searched for items, then reflected that back into the IA.

01

Heuristic analysis

We first evaluated key flows like logging in, registering, and making a purchase using heuristic principles, scoring tasks on clarity and usability. This gave us a baseline of where users struggled. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

02

Card sorting (3 rounds)

We ran a pilot and two main card sorts (5, 6, and 7 participants) using 25–35 cards and 10–15 categories, including a hybrid sort with 18 participants overall. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

03

Tree testing & sitemap iterations

Finally, we ran tree tests with 7 participants to validate findability and used that data to iterate our sitemap through three versions before landing on a final structure. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Research – Card sorting

Learning how people naturally group SHEIN’s world

In our first card sort, we gave participants 35 cards and 10 categories. Broad buckets like “Men,” “Women,” “Kids,” “Pets,” and “Electronics” felt intuitive. The trouble started with items like “Accessories,” “Language,” “Globe,” “Account,” and especially “Family Outfits.” Many people created an “I don’t know where this goes” pile just for that one. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

By the second card sort, we refined the stimuli: 25 cards across 15 categories. We wanted to see if earlier patterns held. 57.1% of participants placed “Kids Accessories” under “Accessories,” which was helpful but not decisive; we ultimately kept it there after looking across all data. Meanwhile, over 85% recommended moving “Language” from “Globe” into “Account,” which became a key IA decision later.

What changed for me: I stopped treating categories as fixed. Once I saw how confidently users disagreed with the original structure, it became easier to let go of legacy labels like “Globe” and re-home content where it truly belonged.
Research – Tree testing

Could people actually find what they needed?

With a draft IA in place, we ran a tree test with 7 participants to see whether the structure held up in practice. We focused on four tasks:

  • Creating a new account.
  • Buying a pet leash.
  • Finding a phone case and screen protector.
  • Finding home decor for Christmas. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

The results told a nuanced story:

  • New account creation: 71.4% success. Most people found “Register” easily, but labels like “Sign Up” and “Login” still overlapped in meaning.
  • Pet leash: 85.7% success. “Accessories” felt like the right category for almost everyone.
  • Phone case & screen protector: 100% success. This area of the IA was already strong.
  • Christmas home decor: 71.4% success, but confusion between “Home Decor” and “Home Textiles” pointed to a labeling problem, not a structural one.
Information architecture

Three sitemap versions, one calmer navigation

We translated our research into three sitemap iterations. Version 1 mirrored the original site with only light changes from the pilot card sort. Version 2 reorganized categories based on Card Sort 1, grouping related items and merging scattered categories. Version 3 absorbed “Globe” into “Account,” gave “Family Outfits” its own page, and clarified ambiguous areas highlighted by tree testing. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

Version 1

Baseline

Based on the pilot card sort, this version mostly exposed the problems: scattered categories, unclear grouping, and long, flat navigation.

Version 2

First re-grouping

We categorized items more logically and merged redundant or overlapping categories, reducing clutter and making navigation feel less arbitrary.

Version 3

Final structure

“Globe” disappeared into “Account,” “Family Outfits” became a dedicated page, and label clarity improved across high-traffic areas like Home and Accessories.

Heuristics & design

From pain points to flows: wireframing key tasks

Our heuristic analysis of two core tasks – “Log in / Register” and “Make a purchase” – scored around 3.1–3.4 out of 5. The flows worked, but they weren’t smooth. Labels were inconsistent, promotions were distracting, and filters felt overwhelming. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

We used those findings to inform wireframes for two redesigned flows:

  • Task 1 – Sign In / Register: A clearer path from “Account” to registration with email/phone, a 6-digit verification code, and options to sign up via Google or Facebook.
  • Task 2 – Make a purchase: A streamlined journey from product selection to checkout, focusing on fewer required fields and clearer options for notifications, order tracking, and promo codes.
Wireframe – Task 1

Sign In / Register

We emphasized trust by adding verification and surfaced social sign-in options in a more predictable location under “Account.”

Wireframe – Task 2

Making a purchase

The checkout flow reduces friction by prioritizing key inputs first, with optional details, promo codes, and notification preferences handled progressively.

Recommendations

What I would hand to a product team

We translated our findings into concrete, prioritized recommendations that connect IA changes to UX improvements. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

01

Clarify and simplify labels

Distinguish clearly between “Sign Up,” “Register,” and “Login,” and refine ambiguous category names like “Home Decor” vs. “Home Textiles” to reduce guesswork.

02

Declutter the interface

Introduce a “hide promotions” or “focus mode” to give users like Emma and Dave a way to shop without constant banners and pop-ups stealing attention.

03

Refine filters & search

Limit filter options to key attributes, add search within filters, and enhance search to handle synonyms and spelling errors more gracefully.

04

Strengthen verification & trust

Add email/SMS verification for critical actions (account changes, payment info) to boost security and allow users to undo mistakes more safely.

05

Highlight guest checkout

Make guest checkout more discoverable and clearly communicate its benefits for new or hesitant users who don’t want to create an account yet.

06

Curated content & smart hierarchy

Introduce sections like “Trending,” “Most Popular,” and “Staff Picks” and apply a clearer content hierarchy so high-value items don’t compete with everything else.

My role & reflection

What I learned as a UX researcher & IA designer

On this project I:

  • Helped plan and run card sorts, including refining instructions and categories between rounds.
  • Participated in tree test design and analysis, focusing on how we framed tasks and interpreted detours.
  • Contributed to drafting each sitemap version and stress-testing it against persona journeys.
  • Co-created wireframes that reflected not just “what looks good,” but what our research suggested users needed.
  • Co-authored the final recommendations and retrospective, articulating how our process could scale to other sites.

This project pushed me to think in systems, not just screens. I came away with a stronger sense of how content strategy, IA, and navigation decisions ripple through the entire user journey – and how research methods like card sorting and tree testing can de-risk those decisions before design and engineering invest heavily.
