Uber Case Study - Improving the Rideshare Experience of Users Who Are Blind or Have Low Vision

A qualitative research project where I worked with blind Uber riders to understand how ridesharing really feels when you can’t see the car coming – and co-designed ways to make each ride safer, clearer, and more independent.

Overview

When “your driver has arrived” isn’t enough

This project started with a simple observation: ridesharing apps were designed assuming the rider can see the car pull up. For riders who are blind or have low vision (BLV), that assumption falls apart quickly.

In class, we had read research documenting how BLV riders are dropped at the wrong place, struggle to locate their car, or have drivers cancel when they hear the word “guide dog.” But we wanted to go deeper: not just collect more stories, but translate those stories into concrete design directions that a rideshare company could realistically act on.

Over 10 weeks, our team:

  • Interviewed four blind Uber riders about their end-to-end experiences.
  • Synthesized their stories into themes using layered qualitative analysis and an affinity map.
  • Co-designed and prioritized solutions like haptic feedback, audio cues, and driver training.
Problem space

Design gap: from “this is hard” to “here’s what we could build”

Existing research already told us that BLV riders face serious barriers, but much of the work stopped at describing pain points. As researchers, we wanted to push the project one step further: use those stories as a starting point to imagine system-level improvements, not just isolated “nice-to-have” features.

We framed our guiding question as:

How might we redesign rideshare experiences so that people who are blind or have low vision can ride independently, confidently, and safely – without having to fight the system every time?

Participants

Who I spoke with

To keep the study focused and rich, we recruited four adults who are completely blind, regularly use Uber, and had taken a rideshare in the last five months. Most lived in the Chicagoland area; one was based in Houston, TX. All were independent travelers who rely on a cane or a guide dog.

Methodology

How I approached the research

We ran one semi-structured interview with each participant over Zoom. I co-moderated sessions with another teammate: one of us led the conversation, while the other monitored time, captured quotes, and watched for moments to probe deeper.

We asked participants to walk us through their typical ride step-by-step – from opening the app to arriving at their destination – and then invited them to share specific stories that felt especially frustrating or memorable. Towards the end of each session, we shifted into “what if” mode and started co-designing.

After data collection, I helped run a layered analysis:

  • We transcribed the interviews and used NotebookLM to quickly surface initial clusters and potential codes.
  • We then moved everything into FigJam, where we manually regrouped quotes and reorganized themes.
  • This became our affinity map – and the backbone for all findings and recommendations.
My lens: I treated the analysis as translation work – moving from emotional, messy stories into patterns that a product team could actually act on, without flattening participants’ lived experiences.
Findings

What I heard from riders, in their own words

As we worked through the affinity map, three themes kept resurfacing, along with cross-cutting app accessibility issues.

Theme 01

Communication breaks down fast

Riders often felt like they were “guessing” what was happening:

  • Drivers who didn’t share a language and relied on pointing at buildings.
  • Silent rides with no confirmation of route or destination.
  • Sudden cancellations with no context.
“He didn’t speak English… he let me off on the other side of the street and I had no idea where I was.”
Theme 02

“Your driver has arrived” is meaningless if you can’t see the car

Pick-up and drop-off were some of the most stressful moments:

  • Generic arrival messages with no direction or landmark.
  • Cars parked on the wrong side of the street or around the corner.
  • Being dropped at “approximately the right place,” which for BLV riders often meant “not usable.”
Theme 03

Bias and lack of disability awareness

Some of the most painful stories weren’t about the app at all – they were about people:

  • Drivers blaming riders for “not seeing” landmarks.
  • Guide-dog refusals or hesitations, despite legal protections.
  • Disbelief when riders described being dropped at the wrong location.
Cross-cutting issue

The app doesn’t always speak screen reader language

Even when the driver was kind, the app itself got in the way:

  • Unlabeled buttons that VoiceOver announced as “button, button, button.”
  • Critical actions like canceling a ride buried in visually obvious, but non-obvious, controls.
  • A general sense that the interface hadn’t been thoroughly tested with BLV users.
Co-designed features

Design ideas that came directly from riders

Rather than brainstorming in isolation, we asked participants, “If you could change the app tomorrow, what would you add or fix?” Four ideas showed up in multiple interviews.

01

Haptic feedback that “points” to the car

Riders wanted a way to feel the car’s arrival even in noisy environments. We proposed distinct vibration patterns that intensify as the vehicle gets closer, plus a clear “arrival” vibration.

“If I can’t hear my phone in the wind or traffic, vibration can tell me the ride is here.”
02

Optional en-route audio updates

Instead of silently watching a map (which riders can’t see), participants asked for spoken cues: major intersections, turns, and confirmations like “Heading north on X, 5 minutes away.”

03

More honest pre-pickup status updates

Riders didn’t mind waiting as much as they minded uncertainty. They suggested messages such as “Driver is delayed at a train crossing” or “Finishing previous drop-off,” instead of a time estimate that never changes.

04

Accessibility training built into the driver experience

Participants wanted drivers to be prepared before the ride, not educated during it. We proposed:

  • Short onboarding modules about working with BLV riders.
  • Clear, scenario-based training on service animals and legal obligations.
  • Simple behavioral guidelines for pick-up and drop-off with BLV riders.
Recommendations

If I were advising a rideshare team, here’s where I’d start

Based on the research and co-designed ideas, we translated our insights into three product-level directions a rideshare company like Uber or Lyft could realistically act on.

01

Build richer, automated communication

Combine push notifications, audio, and haptics to:

  • Explain delays and approach context clearly.
  • Announce arrival in non-visual ways.
  • Offer accessible, pre-written messages drivers can send to riders.
02

Invest in driver support and accountability

Make accessibility part of being a driver:

  • Integrate accessibility expectations into onboarding.
  • Provide concrete examples of do’s and don’ts with BLV riders.
  • Track and address repeated complaints around service-dog refusals.
03

Run a formal accessibility audit of the app

Review core flows like booking, canceling, and finding the driver with BLV users, and fix:

  • Unlabeled controls and poor focus order.
  • Critical actions only obvious visually.
  • Inconsistent or incomplete screen reader support.
Impact & future work

The curb-cut effect: designing for BLV helps everyone

Although this project focused on BLV riders, the proposed changes have a classic “curb-cut effect.” Clearer messaging, better arrival cues, and more predictable driver behavior would also help sighted riders in noisy environments, in unfamiliar cities, or when traveling alone at night.

Our biggest limitation was sample size and composition: four Uber riders, three of whom were older women in the Chicagoland area. In a future iteration, I would:

  • Recruit BLV riders across more cities and transit contexts.
  • Compare experiences between Uber and Lyft.
  • Prototype and usability-test haptic and audio concepts directly with BLV riders.
My role & reflection

What I contributed – and what I took away as a researcher

Across the project, I:

  • Co-moderated interviews, balancing structure with space for storytelling.
  • Worked with the team for affinity mapping and theme definition in FigJam.
  • Translated themes into design directions, constantly asking “How would a product team use this?”
  • Co-authored the final report and helped shape the narrative for our class presentation.

Most importantly, this project shifted the way I think about accessibility work. It’s not a checklist at the end of a project; it’s a way of designing from the perspective of people who are already doing the hard work of adapting. My job as a researcher is to listen carefully, honor their expertise, and then help teams turn those insights into responsible, tangible change.
