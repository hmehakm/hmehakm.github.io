Re-structuring SHEIN’s Navigation for Real People, Not Just Categories

A 10-week case study where our team took SHEIN’s fast-fashion chaos and shaped it into a calmer, more trustworthy shopping journey – using card sorting, tree testing, heuristics, and wireframes to redesign the navigation from the inside out.

About the product

Fast fashion, slow navigation

SHEIN is a global e-commerce platform that ships low-cost fashion to over 200 countries – and adds new items almost every day. That constant growth is great for trends, but rough on navigation. The site’s structure has been stretched over time instead of thoughtfully redesigned, which means shoppers are fighting overlapping categories, vague labels like “Globe,” and a navigation bar that feels more like a maze than a guide.

Our objective in this project was straightforward but ambitious:

  • Create information structures for the navigation bar that match how people actually shop.
  • Remove redundant, confusing categories and reorganize content in a more intuitive way.
  • Design a more secure, trustworthy onboarding and checkout experience.
Personas

Designing for Emma and Dave, not just “users”

We didn’t want to design for a generic “SHEIN shopper.” Instead, we grounded decisions in two personas that represented very different ways of using the site: Emma, a trend-driven fashion shopper, and Dave, a focused electronics buyer. Both were inspired by real behaviors and frustrations we saw in early research.

Testing strategy

Treating IA as a hypothesis, not a given

Rather than treating SHEIN’s existing navigation as “the truth,” we treated it as a hypothesis to test, break apart, and rebuild. Our process combined qualitative and quantitative methods:

01

Heuristic analysis

We evaluated key flows like “Log in / Register” and “Make a purchase” against usability heuristics, which gave us a baseline of where the experience felt confusing, noisy, or risky.

02

Card sorting

Through multiple rounds of open and hybrid card sorts, we learned how people naturally grouped SHEIN’s categories – and where the site’s labels were actively working against them.

03

Tree testing

Finally, we stripped away the UI and tested the bare navigation tree to see whether people could still find what they needed. When they got lost, we treated that as a design bug, not a user error.

Research – Card sorting

Letting users rearrange SHEIN’s world

We started by handing the navigation over to users. In our pilot and two main card sorts, participants grouped 25–35 content cards into categories and were free to rename groups or create new ones. Their decisions told us exactly where the information architecture was fighting their mental model.

Card Sort 1 – Finding the obvious and the awkward

In our first full card sort (35 cards, 10 categories, 5 participants), big categories like Men, Women, Kids, Pets, and Electronics felt intuitive right away. The friction showed up in the gray areas: “Accessories,” “Language,” “Globe,” “Account,” and especially “Family Outfits” often landed in “I don’t know where this goes.”

What this told us: the problem wasn’t just “too many categories.” It was overlapping labels, vague names, and content like Family Outfits that had no clear home.

Card Sort 2 – Refining labels, testing our instincts

For Card Sort 2 (25 cards, 15 categories, 7 participants), we refined the deck based on what we’d already learned. We watched closely for consistency: where did participants agree, and where did they diverge?

  • 57.1% placed “Kids Accessories” under “Accessories” – not unanimous, but consistent with our emerging structure.
  • Over 85% moved “Language” from “Globe” into “Account,” giving us a strong signal to bury “Globe” entirely.
  • “Family Outfits” still stood out as too special to be buried under “Kids” or “Women.”
My lens: Card sorting wasn’t just about tallying percentages; it was about deciding when user disagreement meant “we need clearer labels” versus “this category shouldn’t exist at all.”
Research – Tree testing

Can people find what they need without the UI?

Once we had a draft sitemap, we stripped away all visuals and ran a tree test with 7 participants. The goal: see if the underlying structure could stand on its own. We used contextual inquiries during the test to understand why participants chose certain paths.

Task 1 – Creating a new account

We asked: “If you were to create a new account on SHEIN, where would you go?” 71.4% of participants successfully found the “Register” path, most of them directly. The rest gravitated toward labels like “Login” or got distracted by alternative entry points.

Task 2 – Finding a pet leash

When we asked where they’d look for a pet leash, 85.7% of participants went to “Accessories,” with one minor detour. This told us the category was doing its job – we didn’t need a full redesign, just subtle label tuning.

Task 3 – Phone case & screen protector

This was our strongest moment: 100% of participants successfully found the phone case and screen protector categories. Five went there directly, and two took short detours.

Task 4 – Christmas home decor

When we asked participants to find Christmas home decor, 71.4% succeeded – but confusion between “Home Decor” and “Home Textiles” was obvious. People hesitated, guessed, or second-checked their choices.

My takeaway: tree testing gave us permission to stop guessing. If users failed in a stripped-down tree, we knew the fix was structural or linguistic – not something we could patch with UI chrome.
Information architecture

From scattered categories to a calmer sitemap

Using insights from card sorting and tree testing, we iterated through three sitemap versions. Each version pulled the structure closer to how Emma and Dave actually think when they shop:

Version 1

Baseline

Closely mirrored the original site plus pilot card sort insights. Useful mainly to expose how scattered and redundant some sections were.

Version 2

Re-grouped

Informed by Card Sort 1 & 2. We merged overlapping categories, re-homed edge cases, and started carving out a clearer hierarchy.

Version 3

Final IA

“Globe” disappeared into “Account,” “Family Outfits” became a dedicated page, and high-traffic areas like Home and Accessories were clarified.

Highlight: giving “Family Outfits” a real home

Across every card sort, “Family Outfits” confused people. It never really belonged under “Kids” alone, and users treated it as a special destination – almost like a campaign page.

Highlight: retiring the “Globe” page

The “Globe” page tried to hold settings like language and region, but users repeatedly placed “Language” under “Account” during card sorts. Instead of fighting that, we embraced it.

Heuristic analysis

Zooming in on log in and checkout

While IA work reshaped the big picture, heuristic analysis helped us focus on two critical flows: Log In / Register and Make a Purchase. Both flows worked – but not gracefully. We scored them around 3.1–3.4 out of 5.

We repeatedly saw:

  • Inconsistent labels such as “Sign Up,” “Register,” and “Log In” used interchangeably.
  • Visual noise from banners and promotions during sensitive steps like checkout.
  • Filters and search that tried to do everything at once, instead of focusing on key attributes.
Wireframes

Translating research into flows

With a cleaner IA and a clear list of usability issues, we designed low-fidelity wireframes for two key tasks: signing in / registering and making a purchase. The goal wasn’t pixel-perfect UI – it was to tell a more coherent story for Emma and Dave.

Task 1

Sign In / Register

The flow starts from the “Account” button and leads to a combined Sign In / Register screen. New users can enter an email or phone number and receive a 6-digit verification code, or use Google / Facebook for a faster path.

The emphasis is on trust: fewer surprises, clearer steps, and built-in verification for security.

Task 2

Making a purchase

The purchase flow begins on the product page: the user chooses size, color, and quantity, then adds the item to the cart. At checkout, we prioritize essentials – address, payment, and confirmation – and keep extras like promo codes or notification choices secondary.

After purchase, users can choose how they’re notified, view order details, or access a barcode within the app, supporting both Emma’s browsing style and Dave’s efficiency.

Recommendations

What I’d hand to a product team

We translated our research into practical, prioritized recommendations that connect IA and UX decisions directly to business outcomes like faster discovery, higher trust, and smoother checkout.

01

Consistent, clear labeling

Standardize terms like “Sign Up,” “Register,” and “Log In,” and differentiate similar categories such as “Home Decor” vs. “Home Textiles” to reduce guesswork.

02

Declutter key flows

Introduce a “hide promotions” or “focus mode” toggle so shoppers can complete registration and checkout without pop-ups and banners competing for attention.

03

Refined filters & search

Simplify the filter bar to focus on key attributes and add search within filters. Improve search to recognize synonyms and handle spelling errors gracefully.

04

Stronger verification

Add email/SMS verification for critical actions (account updates, payment changes) so users feel safer and can recover from mistakes more easily.

05

Guest checkout that feels intentional

Highlight guest checkout as a legitimate option, not an afterthought, especially for new or privacy-conscious shoppers.

06

Curated content & hierarchy

Add sections like “Trending,” “Most Popular,” and “Staff Picks,” and establish a clear content hierarchy so high-value items stand out instead of competing with everything else.

My role & reflection

Thinking in systems, not just screens

Throughout this project, I moved between research and structure – always asking, “How will this decision show up in the real shopping experience?”

  • Co-led planning and facilitation for card sorts, including refining instructions and card sets between rounds.
  • Helped design the tree test tasks and interpret direct vs. indirect paths, treating detours as clues rather than noise.
  • Contributed to each sitemap version and tested changes against Emma and Dave’s journeys.
  • Co-created wireframes focused on clarity and trust, not just aesthetics.
  • Co-authored the final recommendations and retrospective, documenting how this process can scale to other complex catalogs.

Most importantly, this project strengthened my habit of treating navigation as a living system. When thousands of products are changing every week, IA isn’t a one-time deliverable – it’s an ongoing conversation between user expectations, content growth, and business goals.
